Description:
To extend the torque neural network made by a fellow classmate, I made a deep spatial autoencoder based on the work by Chelsea Finn et al. The goal was to take a blind robot and give it vision by automatically constructing the state-space directly from camera images and perform visuomotor learning. It takes an image and encodes the positions of relevant objects in the world using Cartesian coordinates, generating a state-space. Over time, as thxe objects move, the coordinates change. The autoencoder takes the full-resolution RGB image and generate a low-dimensional, dense vector. Unlike most autoencoders, this one encodes spatial feature locations instead of the actual feature values, as a way of representing the objectâ€™s location. This can be used as a representation of the state-space and is trained simultaneously with a neural network with an additional loss function. This was part of a larger group project.

Use of Synthetic Images in Training Convolutional Neural Networks

Repository:
Private
With Whom:
Cornellâ€™s Graphics and Vision group
When:
Fall 2015 â€“ Spring 2016
Paper:
Document
Description:
We built and trained a deep network to learn an embedding using synthesized image data generated using iconic images of products and various backgrounds to see if synthesized data can improve the network. Training a deep convolutional neural network (CNN) requires a lot of images, which can be expensive and time-consuming to acquire. We attempted to obtain feasible results with our synthesized data, which can be generated cheaply and efficiently. We used iconic images of the products as ''anchors'' in our embedding, and synthesized new images by compositing the iconic image onto a background photograph. For training the network, we generated pairs of positive and negative examples for each iconic image with the synthetic images, and evaluated progress with both a triplet loss function and contrastive loss. We used GoogLeNet to generate the embedding for each loss function.We attempted to use the triplet loss and contrastive loss to separate dissimilar embeddings and cluster similar embeddings, both of which measure similarity with Euclidean distances. Our ultimate goal was to show that training a CNN to learn an embedding can be made cost-effective in time and monetary resources while still achieving high classification accuracy.

Reimplementation of Coal-HMM to Analyze Speciation Times of Humans, Chimpanzees and Gorillas

Repository:
Github
With Whom:
CS 4775 â€“ Computational Genetics and Genomics
When:
Spring 2015
Paper:
Document
Description:
Although we know the genetic relationship between humans, chimpanzees, gorillas and orangutans and their speciation times, a debated topic in genomics is their zzzzdivergence times. Understanding when species diverged would help understand how the ancestral species interacted with each other, if they even did. We know that orangutans split first, then gorillas, and lastly chimpanzees, leaving humans as their own species. The way they diverged can be represented in 4 different states: HC1, HC2, HG, and CG. HC1 suggests some dividing force like a river separating the humans and the chimpanzees while the remaining three states suggest that humans and chimpanzees experiences similar selective pressures, forcing their speciation time to differ from their divergence time. In order to determine which state is the most likely, I implemented a Coalescent HMM. This is a reimplementation of the algorithm in "Genomic Relationships and Speciation Times of Human, Chimpanzee, and Gorilla Inferred from a Coalescent Hidden Markov Model" by Hobolth et al.

Analysis of Anti-Vaccine Movement through Social Media

Repository:
Github
With Whom:
Data Science Hackathon
When:
March 8, 2015
Description:
My team and I wanted to see what we could learn about the anti-vaccine movement through social media, specifically Twitter. We collected tweets from a 10 day period related to the movement and created a network graph of the users, hashtags, and linked websites. Nodes were connected if the users followed each other, a user mentioned a topic, or two topics were in the same tweet. As we were trying to identify the two sides of the movement (for and against), we clustered the graph using spectral clustering. To actually learn about the two movements, we created summaries of the tweets, hashtags, and linked websites for both sides. We were hoping to see if either side had a scientific base. We learned that the two sides are connected, so there was a bit of overlap in the summaries. Overall, this was an interesting study to see the role of social media in present day movements.
